What is ransomware?

Ransomware is malware (computer software that is intentionally designed to cause harm to a network(s), a device(s), or a user(s) which encrypts (scrambles and locks) targeted files, thereby making the devices and networks dependent on those files inoperable. The victims of ransomware are often businesses who have both sensitive data in their computer networks and access to funds that would make the operation most lucrative to cybercriminals. The recent Colonial Oil Pipeline ransomware attack has brought a new spotlight to this dark world where neither the suspect nor the victim wants to be known.

One of the most lucrative ransomwares, Ryuk, provides an instructive example of how ransomware works. Ryuk, like a lot of malware is delivered through spam e-mails from fabricated addresses that appear reputable (Malwarebytes, 2021). Malwarebytes.com explains the typical delivery process:

A typical Ryuk attack begins when a user opens a weaponized Microsoft Office document attached to a phishing email. Opening the document causes a malicious macro to execute a PowerShell command that attempts to download the banking Trojan Emotet. This Trojan has the ability to download additional malware onto an infected machine that retrieves and executes Trickbot, of which the main payload is spyware. This collects admin credentials, allowing attackers to move laterally to critical assets connected to the network. The attack chain concludes when the attackers execute Ryuk on each of these assets (Malwarebytes, 2021).

The delivery process ends when the Ryuk encrypts the files, and a ransom message is delivered to the victims. Then, the victim is forced to decide, and while law enforcement discourages victims from paying the ransom, many have no choice but to pay just like Colonial Pipeline Company recently did. In fact, the Colonial episode highlights the power of ransomware. The United States Government, with all its resources, crack the malware. They could only wait for Colonial to settle with the hackers before seeking retaliation against hacking group

The best course of action for an individual or organization can take in dealing with the threat of ransomware is prevention. The first step in a prevention program is installing and utilizing an anti-virus/anti-malware software. Companies like Malwarebytes sell software that reportedly can block ransomware from encrypting files and detecting whether any of the holes have been created in your network that hackers can exploit at any time. It is important to note that no anti-virus/anti-malware program can prevent attacks if it is not installed and updated on all network devices. Perhaps the most important measure of ransomware prevention is education and training. All network users need to be made aware of the threat of ransomware and that it is often deployed through spam emails with infected attached documents. Many users will scoff at the possibility that they would open spam and an infected attachment, but when you are part of a network, you are only as safe as your weakest link.
